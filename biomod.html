SynthePHERE

Abstract

Under appropriate temperature and ionic environments, DNA spontaneously forms a double helix structure based on base complementarity. In recent years, by taking advantage of this property and folding long circular single-stranded DNA (scaffold) into hundreds of short single-stranded DNA (staples), two-dimensional nanoscale structures or three-dimensional nanoscale structures with a size of approximately 100 nm have been created. A technology called DNA origami to create structures is attracting attention (1)(2). Furthermore, by linking multiple DNA origami to form a multimer, complex and functional larger-scale structures can be created (3) (4). Among the DNA origami multimers obtained in this way, this research focuses on two-dimensional arrays that combine multiple two-dimensional structures. Two-dimensional arrays using DNA origami have various applications. For example, by taking advantage of the property that DNA can be modified with nanostructures, we have prepared a sample in which proteins called ion channels are arranged at high density on the surface of a two-dimensional array, and applied it to X-ray single molecule measurement (56) to detect ion channels. If dynamic observation is performed, statistical evaluation under the same conditions becomes possible. Similarly, gold nanoparticle dimers used in surface-enhanced Raman scattering (SERS) (7) can be arranged with high precision and high density. Furthermore, it can also be used as a mask for nanolithography (8)-(11). However, currently, the size of two-dimensional DNA origami arrays is only a few micrometers square, and there are also local defects and deformations, so they cannot be used in applications that require geometric precision. In particular, in order to ensure the mechanical rigidity of the two-dimensional array structure and to bond between DNA origami with high precision without any gaps, single-stranded DNA is used to cross-link the double helix structures that make up each of the two DNA origami. A method called crossover is suitable (12)(13). Single-stranded DNA used for this purpose is called a linker. However, this method has not yet yielded two-dimensional arrays of sufficient size and yield, which remains a problem to be solved. In this study, we focused on a system in which equilateral triangular DNA origami (hereinafter referred to as triangular DNA origami) are connected with linkers to form a two-dimensional array. The triangular DNA origami was designed to be suitable for two-dimensional array formation, and the length of one side is approximately 114 mm (Figure 1). For adjacent triangular DNA origami, connect the same sides A, B, and C, which are distinguished by markers, with the linker corresponding to that side. Therefore, it is possible to selectively form dimers in which two triangular DNA origami are linked, multimers linked in one dimension, and multimers in the form of a two-dimensional array (Figure 2). As shown in Figure 3, there are four types of linkers each corresponding to the A, B, and C sides, and a total of eight linkers, two of each type, bridge the double helices of the two triangular DNA origami. For cross-linking, we used a crossover method that uses two linkers at one location. In the dimer production of triangular DNA origami performed in our laboratory, the dimer formation rate on two of the three sides was significantly lower (Figure 4(a)). This triangular DNA origami is the second design origami, and the one before the redesign is the first design origami. However, in the dimer production experiment of the second designed origami, the proportion of dimer formation on one of the three sides was significantly lower (Figure 4(b)). This factor could not be explained by the presence of staples with sequences identical or complementary to the linker, or the influence of secondary structure. Therefore, in this study, we aimed to elucidate the governing factors in the dimer formation of triangular DNA origami using linkers using an approach different from the method described above. First, we focused on the shape of the triangular DNA origami and analyzed it using the structural analysis tool "CanDo" (2). By comparing this with the results of dimer production experiments, we hypothesized the factors governing the dimer formation rate. We also conducted dimer production experiments using newly designed triangular DNA origami and linkers to verify the validity of our assumptions.
