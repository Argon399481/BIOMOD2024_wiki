Project

DNA spontaneously forms double helix structures based on base complementarity under appropriate temperature and ionic conditions. In recent years, a technology called DNA origami has attracted attention, which uses this property to fold long cyclic single-stranded DNA (scaffold) with hundreds of short single-stranded DNA (staple) to produce two-dimensional or three-dimensional nanoscale structures with a size of approximately 100 nm[1][2]. By combining multiple DNA origami and making them into polymers, it is possible to fabricate larger-scale structures with complex functionality [3][4].

In this study, we focus on two-dimensional arrays in which multiple two-dimensional structures are combined from among the multimeric forms of DNA origami obtained in this way. Two-dimensional arrays using DNA origami can be used for various applications. For example, using the property that nanostructures can be modified on DNA, a sample with a high-density array of proteins called ion channels on the surface of a two-dimensional array can be prepared, and if ion channel dynamics can be observed by applying X-ray single-molecule measurement methods [5][6], statistical evaluation under the same conditions will be possible. In the same way, gold nanoparticle dimers for SERS (Surface-Enhanced Raman Scattering) [7] can be precisely and densely arrayed. Furthermore, they can be used as masks for nanolithography [8][9][10][11]. However, the size of 2D arrays of DNA origami is currently limited to a few µm square, and the existence of partial defects and deformations precludes their use in applications where geometric precision is required. To ensure the mechanical rigidity of a two-dimensional array structure, a method called “crossover” using single-stranded DNA to crosslink the double helix structures of two DNA origamis is particularly suited for precise, gapless joining. The single-stranded DNA used for this purpose is called a linker. However, this method has not yet produced two-dimensional arrays of sufficient size and yield, which is a problem to be solved.

In this study, a system in which equilateral triangular DNA origami (hereafter referred to as triangular DNA origami) are joined by a linker to form two-dimensional arrays was targeted. The triangular DNA origami was designed to be suitable for two-dimensional arrays, with each side measuring approximately 114 mm (Fig. 1).

triangular DNA origami is joined to the same side of the A, B and C sides by a linker corresponding to that side, which is identified by a marker. Therefore, it is possible to selectively form a dimer consisting of two triangular DNA origami joined together, a multimer consisting of a one-dimensional linkage, and a two-dimensional array of multimers, respectively (Fig. 2). There are four types of linkers, each corresponding to the A, B, and C edges, and two types of linkers each, for a total of eight types, are used to bridge the double helix of two triangular DNA origami sheets (Figure 3).

A crossover technique was used for crosslinking, in which two linkers are used at one location. In the dimerization of triangular DNA origami in our laboratory, the dimerization rate on two of the three sides was significantly lower (Fig. 4(a)). In DNA base pairs, the binding strength of C (cytosine)-G (guanine) was greater than that of A (adenine)-T (thymine), and due to the correlation with the dimer formation rate, a triangular DNA origami was redesigned by increasing the number of C and G bases in the linker sequence. This triangular DNA origami is referred to as the second design origami, and the one before the redesign is the first design origami. However, the dimer formation rate on one of the three sides of the second design origami was significantly lower in the dimer production experiments (Fig. 4(b)).

This factor could not be explained by the presence of staples with sequences identical or complementary to the linker or by the influence of secondary structure. Therefore, the present study aimed to clarify the dominant factor in the dimer formation of triangular DNA origami with a linker using an approach different from that described above. First, the shape of the triangular DNA origami was analyzed using the structural analysis tool "CanDo"[2]. By comparing this with the results of dimer production experiments, the dominant factor in the dimer formation rate was assumed. The validity of the assumption was verified by conducting dimer formation experiments using newly designed triangular DNA origami and linkers.
